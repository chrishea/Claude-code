üö® CRITICAL ISSUES CHECK

1. SOURCING VERIFICATION

FATAL FLAWS - Cannot publish without addressing:
  • Are ALL factual claims attributed to specific sources?
  • Are sources named and identifiable?
  • Are sources credible and appropriate for the claims made?
  • Are there any unsourced claims that appear to be factual?
  • Do quotes have proper attribution?
  • Are secondhand sources clearly identified as such?

2. AI-GENERATED TEXT DETECTION

RED FLAGS - High probability AI content indicators:
  • Generic promotional language: "miraculous," "tremendous success," "state-of-the-art," "cutting-edge," "game-changer"
  • Smooth sourceless narrative: Confident assertions without attribution
  • Editorializing without attribution: Subjective conclusions presented as fact
  • Formulaic conclusions: "Legacy persists," "testament to," "continues to this day"
  • Vague but impressive-sounding claims: Numbers or details without specific sources
  • Lack of specific quotes: Paraphrasing instead of direct quotes from real people
  • Overly celebratory tone: Uncritical praise without nuance
  • Perfect storytelling: No complications, contradictions, or messy details
  • Absence of journalistic voice: Reads like marketing copy or Wikipedia

ACTION REQUIRED: If multiple indicators present, verify with original reporting or clearly label as unverified/historical account.

‚úì FACT-CHECKING PROTOCOL

Verification Requirements

  • Names: Verify spelling, titles, and credentials of all people mentioned
  • Dates: Confirm all dates, times, and timelines
  • Numbers: Verify statistics, financial figures, measurements, ages
  • Locations: Confirm addresses, place names, geographic details
  • Organizations: Verify names, acronyms, and descriptions
  • Historical claims: Cross-reference with reliable historical sources
  • Technical information: Verify specialized or technical details with experts

Red Flags Requiring Extra Scrutiny

  • Claims that seem too perfect or convenient
  • Round numbers without explanation
  • Superlatives ("first," "only," "best," "worst")
  • Claims that advance a narrative too neatly
  • Information that contradicts common knowledge
  • Details that can't be easily verified

Missing Critical Context

  • What's the other side of the story?
  • What complications or failures existed?
  • What information contradicts the main narrative?
  • Who disagrees with the claims made?
  • What documentation exists to support claims?

üì∞ AP STYLE COMPLIANCE

Common AP Style Issues to Check

  • Dates: Month day, year (Jan. 15, 2024) - Spell out months in text
  • Times: Use figures except for noon and midnight (3 p.m., 10:30 a.m.)
  • Numbers: Spell out one through nine, use figures for 10 and above
  • Addresses: Abbreviate Ave., Blvd., St. only with numbered address
  • States: Spell out in body text, abbreviate in datelines
  • Titles: Capitalize before names, lowercase after
  • Hyphenation: "around-the-clock" not "round-the-clock"; "multigenerational" (no hyphen)
  • Percentages: Use figures and spell out "percent" (15 percent)
  • Money: Use figures and $ sign ($5, $1 million)
  • Ages: Always use figures (5-year-old, 55 years old)

Local Style Preferences

  • Cloudcroft Village Council (not "Town Council" or "City Council")
  • Otero County (verify county references)
  • Highway 82, U.S. 70 (not "Hwy" or "Route")

HIGH RISK - Requires legal review:
  • Accusations of criminal activity
  • Claims of unethical professional conduct
  • Allegations of fraud, corruption, or dishonesty
  • Statements that could damage reputation or livelihood
  • Claims about private life or character
MEDIUM RISK - Requires careful sourcing:
  • Criticism of public officials' performance
  • Reports of investigations or complaints
  • Financial difficulties of businesses
  • Disputes between parties

  • ‚úì Are opinions clearly labeled as such?

Required for Controversial Claims

üìù EDITORIAL STANDARDS

Core Journalistic Requirements

  1. News Hook: Why are we telling this story now? What makes it timely?
  2. Original Reporting: What did we discover ourselves? What's new?
  3. Multiple Perspectives: Have we included all relevant viewpoints?
  4. Critical Analysis: Have we challenged assumptions and tested claims?
  5. Complete Story: Who, what, when, where, why, how all addressed?
  6. Context: Does the reader have enough background to understand?

Tone and Voice

  • ‚úì Professional, not promotional
  • ‚úì Fair and balanced, not advocacy
  • ‚úì Clear and accessible, not jargon-heavy
  • ‚úì Engaging, not dry
  • ‚úì Local focus, not generic
  • ‚úì Respectful, not sensational

  • No quotes from real people
  • One-sided presentation

üìö WRITING CRAFT REFERENCE

Top principles from leading writing guides to elevate prose quality and storytelling.

üìò Writing Tools ‚Äî Roy Peter Clark

  • Front-load meaning: Favor short, clear sentences with strong subjects and active verbs; make meaning early, then let details branch to the right.
  • Vary rhythm: Vary sentence length for rhythm, and "climb the ladder of abstraction" by mixing concrete detail (names, numbers, scenes) with a few well-chosen general statements.
  • Control pacing: Slow down on key moments with specific sensory detail and quotes; speed up on routine background.
  • Cut clutter: Remove throat-clearing, redundancies, and filler adverbs; choose precise nouns and verbs instead.

üìó Art and Craft of Feature Writing ‚Äî William Blundell

  • Theme statement first: Start every story by writing a one- or two-sentence theme statement that captures what the piece is really about; use it to decide what to keep and what to cut.
  • Find fresh angles: Look at ideas from multiple "story dimensions" (history, scope, impact, process, people, data) to find the freshest angles.
  • Plan loosely, write tightly: Work from a loose plan: outline major sections, turning points, and key scenes before drafting, then keep digressions short and return quickly to the main action.
  • End with resonance: End by giving the reader something new to think about so the story feels larger than the incident that triggered it.

üìô Major Newsroom Style Guides (NYT, WSJ, BBC)

  • Consistency over cleverness: Use a consistent style standard (AP plus a house style layer) for capitalization, numbers, titles, and sensitive terms; when in doubt, choose clarity over cleverness.
  • Neutral, specific language: Prefer neutral, specific language over labels or loaded terms, especially in politics, crime, and social issues.
  • Headlines that inform: Write headlines that are accurate, concrete, and free of hype; avoid puns unless they clarify rather than obscure.
  • Clean modern voice: Maintain a clean, modern news voice: active, declarative sentences, minimal jargon, and attribution that makes sourcing transparent.

üìï Ernest Hemingway ‚Äî Prose Principles

  • Short sentences: Use short sentences. They punch. They move.
  • Active voice: Write in active voice. Subject acts on object. No hiding.
  • Iceberg theory: Show, don't tell. The dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water. Let readers feel what's beneath.
  • Stop while you know: Stop writing while you still know what happens next. You'll have momentum tomorrow.
  • One true sentence: Write one true sentence. The truest sentence you know. Then go from there.
  • Edit ruthlessly: The first draft of anything is shit. Rewrite it. Then rewrite it again.
  • Strong verbs: Use strong, concrete verbs. Verbs do the work. Let them.
  • Kill modifiers: Avoid unnecessary adjectives and adverbs. If the noun is right, it doesn't need propping up.
  • Concrete detail: Ground your writing in concrete detail and observation. Specific beats general every time.
  • Write what hurts: Write hard and clear about what hurts. That's where the truth lives.
  • Actions reveal character: Don't judge your characters; let actions reveal them. Trust your reader.
  • Earn every word: Strip prose down to the bone so every word earns its place. If it doesn't work, cut it.

PUBLISH AS-IS

PUBLISH WITH MINOR REVISIONS

AP style corrections needed, or minor fact-checking required, but no fundamental issues with sourcing or editorial standards.

DO NOT PUBLISH WITHOUT MAJOR REVISION

Required when story has:

  • Sourcing problems
  • Significant AI-generated content concerns
  • Fact-checking failures
  • Potential libel issues
  • Editorial standards violations

ACTION REQUIRED: List specific fixes needed before story can be reconsidered.

KILL STORY

Fatal flaws that cannot be corrected:

  • Core facts cannot be verified
  • Sources unreliable or fabricated
  • Serious libel risk with insufficient defense
  • Story fundamentally misleading
  • Ethical violations in reporting
